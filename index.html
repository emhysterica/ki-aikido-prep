Four basic principles
In Ki Aikido, technique follows from mind-body unification. All four principles are ultimately one.
1
Keep one point
Settle your awareness and weight into your seika tanden — just below the navel. This is your physical and mental center of gravity. A calm, collected mind at one point makes your body stable and responsive rather than stiff or scattered.
In technique: a centered nage cannot be easily pushed off balance — uke's attack finds nothing to push against.
2
Relax completely
True relaxation is not limpness — it is the absence of unnecessary tension. A relaxed body moves faster, feels uke's energy more clearly, and does not telegraph intention. Muscular force creates conflict; relaxed ki moves through and around it.
In technique: gripping, bracing, or forcing a throw breaks connection with uke. Let the throw happen rather than making it happen.
3
Keep weight underside
Relax the weight of every part of your body to its natural underside — like water settling. This creates rootedness without effort. Arms held with weight underside feel substantial to uke without any muscular push.
In technique: arms extended with weight underside are difficult to lift or resist. This is the physical foundation of kokyunage and most projections.
4
Extend ki
Ki extends naturally from a unified mind and body. Think of light from a lamp — it does not stop at the bulb. Your awareness and intention extend beyond your fingertips, beyond the mat. Extending ki through uke — not to them — leads them naturally rather than pushing or pulling.
Zanshin is the continuation of extended ki after the throw. It is not a pose — it is sustained awareness.
Five principles of Shin Shin Toitsu Aikido
1. Ki is extending
2. Know your partner's mind
3. Respect your partner's ki
4. Put yourself in your partner's place
5. Perform with confidence

What to expect
The examiner observes not just your mechanics but your ki — calmness, extension, and connection with uke throughout.
Before you begin
Settle into one point. Take a breath. The test starts in your mind before it starts on the mat. Examiners notice whether you are centered and present, not just whether footwork is correct.
1 — Kumiwaza (24 techniques)
The examiner calls each technique by name. Blend and lead — never meet force with force. Both irimi and tenkan required where listed. Maintain zanshin after each throw.
2 — Jiyu waza (free techniques)
5 throws of your choice for each of 4 attacks: Yokomenuchi, Katatetori, Ushirotekubitori, and Tantotori (knife). Vary your techniques. Stay centered and lead uke rather than reacting.
3 — Bokken 1: Happo Giri
Eight-direction sword cutting kata, performed solo. Each cut should express extended ki, not just arm movement. Keep one point through every transition.
4 — Jo 1
First jo kata. Relaxed grip, weight underside, ki extending through the tip of the staff on every strike and thrust.
Etiquette
Bow in with sincerity. If you make an error — reset with one point and continue. Do not apologize mid-test. The examiner wants to see that your mind stays calm under pressure.
